  "summary": "The Meru (Ameru) are a Bantu ethnic group inhabiting the fertile north and eastern slopes of Mount Kenya in Meru and Tharaka-Nithi counties. Numbering approximately 2.5 million (2019 census), they comprise nine subgroups: Igoji, Imenti, Tigania, Mitine, Igembe, Mwimbi, Muthambi, Chuka, and Tharaka. The Meru possess one of East Africa's most detailed and richly preserved oral migration traditions, tracing their origin to Mbwaa (Manda Island) on the Kenyan coast, where they were subjugated by Nguo Ntuni invaders (likely Arabs or Persian-influenced coastal traders). A dramatic escape involving ritual specialists (Koome Njoe, Mururia), impossible tasks, and coordinated flight under prophetic guidance led them on a multi-generational trek through Kitui lowlands, Tana River systems, and arid plains before ascending Mount Kenya. Upon arrival, they displaced or assimilated Cushitic (Muoko, Ukara), Kalenjin (Umpua, Agumba), and Maa-speaking (Il Tikirri, Maasai) populations, absorbing significant Nilotic cultural influence—particularly in age-set organization, military structure, and the adoption of the name 'Meru' itself from Maasai. The Meru are governed by a hierarchical council of elders system culminating in the Njuri-Ncheke, the supreme traditional judicial and legislative body, which remains active in cultural governance, land conservation, and higher education (donating land for Meru University of Science and Technology). The community produced Kenya's first African coffee farmers (1930s) and continues to exert significant political influence, producing the current Deputy President (Kithure Kindiki) and Chief Justice (Martha Koome).",
