  "summary": "The Embu (Aembu) are a Bantu ethnic group indigenous to Embu County on the southern windward slopes of Mount Kenya, forming part of the GEMA (Gikuyu-Embu-Meru-Akamba) cultural and linguistic cluster. Numbering approximately 608,599 (2019 census, Embu County; plus Mbeere subgroup), they comprise two subgroups: the highland Embu proper and the semi-arid lowland Mbeere. Oral tradition traces their origin to Mwenendega, a primordial man created by Ngai (God) at Mwenendega grove (near Runyenjes), who discovered a mysterious woman, Ciũrũnjĩ/Nthara, at Gogo River Salt Lick. Their descendants populated Embu after a divine flood punished Mwenendega for breaking his oath. Linguistic and migration evidence suggests coastal origins (Mpwa/Pwani), with retreat northwest due to conflicts with Swahili and Mijikenda communities, followed by settlement on Mount Kenya's slopes—which they named after Nyaga/Njerũ (snow/whiteness), hence Mwenenyaga ('Owner of the Snow'). Historically, Embu were fierce warriors defending their territory against Kamba and Maasai invasions, though their small population limited regional impact. They actively participated in the Mau Mau uprising against British colonial rule. Senior Chief Muruatetu (16 wives, numerous descendants) exemplifies pre-colonial wealth (wives/children as riches) and colonial-era collaboration. The Embu are primarily agrarian, cultivating coffee, tea, muguka (khat), and macadamia as cash crops on some of Kenya's most fertile highlands, resulting in Embu County consistently ranking among Kenya's wealthiest counties with the longest life expectancy. Notable figures include Justin Muturi (Speaker of National Assembly 2013-2022), Cardinal John Njue, and Governor Cecily Mutitu Mbarire.",
