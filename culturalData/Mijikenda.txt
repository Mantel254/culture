  "summary": "The Mijikenda are a confederation of nine related Bantu ethnic groups inhabiting the coastal hinterland of Kenya, between the Sabaki and Umba rivers, from the Tanzania border north toward Somalia. Numbering approximately 2.5 million (2019 census, aggregated), the nine constituent groups are: Chonyi, Kambe, Duruma, Kauma, Ribe, Rabai, Jibana, Giriama, and Digo. The Digo are classified as 'southern Mijikenda' and also reside in Tanzania; the other eight are 'northern Mijikenda'. Historically known as the 'Nyika' by outsiders—a pejorative term meaning 'bush people'—the Mijikenda maintained fortified hilltop settlements called kaya (plural: makaya) from the 17th century onward, which served as political, spiritual, and defensive centers. Eleven of these kaya forests are collectively inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site (Sacred Mijikenda Kaya Forests). Mijikenda origin traditions trace their ancestry to Shungwaya (Singwaya) on the northern Somali coast, from which they were displaced southward by Oromo (Galla) expansion c. 16th century—a narrative that serves both as historical memory and as a charter for ethnic unity. The Mijikenda are known for wooden vigango funerary statues, which entered the international art market controversially in the 1970s–1990s. During the colonial period, Mijikenda (particularly Giriama) experienced enslavement by Arab and Swahili traders, followed by complex patron-client relations and resistance to British administration.",
