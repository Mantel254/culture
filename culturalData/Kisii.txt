  "summary": "The Gusii (Abagusii) are a Bantu ethnic group indigenous to Kisii and Nyamira counties in western Kenya, with significant populations in Kericho and Bomet counties. They number 2.7 million (2019 census), making them Kenya's sixth-largest ethnicity. The Gusii speak Ekegusii, a Great Lakes Bantu language with distinctive tense structures setting it apart from neighboring Bantu languages. Their oral traditions recount a staged migration from 'Misri' (often associated with Egypt), through eastern Uganda, Mount Elgon, the Yimbo area, Kisumu, and Kano, before final settlement in the Kisii highlands after pressure from Luo and passage through Kalenjin/Maasai territory. The Gusii are renowned for their soapstone sculptures (chigware, Tabaka), the eight-stringed bass lyre (obokano—the largest such lyre in Kenya), and their historic reputation as formidable warriors. Colonial encounters were marked by punitive expeditions, suppression of cattle camps (ebisarate), and forced settlement dispersal (1913). The community maintains a decentralized, clan-based governance structure and practices male circumcision as a central rite of passage. Contemporary Gusii have significant diaspora populations in the United States (notably Minnesota), United Kingdom, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, and South Africa.",
